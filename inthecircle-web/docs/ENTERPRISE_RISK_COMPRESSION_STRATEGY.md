# Enterprise Risk Compression Strategy

Use governance maturity to shorten enterprise sales cycles and increase close probability.

---

## SECTION 1 — Discovery Call Positioning

**Script for introducing governance in the first 15 minutes**

---

**Opening (first 2–3 min)**  
“Before we go into [product/capabilities], one thing that’s helped our enterprise customers is how we handle governance. Can I spend a couple of minutes on that?”

**Frame 1 — Designed in from day one**  
“We designed the platform with institutional governance from day one — not bolted on after the fact. That means role segregation, approval workflows for high-impact actions, and a tamper-evident audit trail are part of the product. Your security and compliance teams get the same story we give auditors.”

**Frame 2 — Security review rarely blocks**  
“In practice, security review rarely blocks our deals. Not because we skip steps — because we’ve already got the structure and evidence paths. We have a public Trust Center, evidence-on-demand by control code, and a live control health view. So the review tends to be validation, not discovery.”

**Frame 3 — Live walkthrough**  
“We can walk your team through controls live — Trust Center, audit chain, 4-eyes flow, and the control health dashboard. Usually 20–30 minutes. That often replaces a long questionnaire cycle.”

**Handoff**  
“If that’s useful, we can slot that in early and then go deep on [use case]. Does your team usually own security review, or is it central?”

## SECTION 2 — Risk Reversal Narrative

**Language that reduces perceived vendor risk and signals operational discipline**

---

**Vendor risk reduction**

- “We don’t ask you to take our word for it. Trust Center is public; evidence is generated by control code; control health is visible in admin. You can verify before you commit.”
- “We built for the review you’re going to run anyway. So your risk is that the review takes time — not that we can’t support it.”
- “Our standard is: if we can’t show it or export it, we don’t claim it. No certification claims we don’t hold; no controls we can’t demonstrate.”

**Operational discipline**

- “Governance is in the product — RBAC, 4-eyes, tamper-evident audit, continuous control checks. That’s operational discipline, not policy theater.”
- “We run control health daily. Escalations, overdue items, and drift show up in the same place. So we’re not scrambling when you ask for evidence.”
- “Destructive actions require a second approval. Audit is append-only and hash-chained. That’s how we run; it’s not a special mode for enterprise.”

**Competitor fragility (indirect)**

- “Some vendors add governance after the fact — you end up waiting for features or relying on policy docs. We started with the control model, so your timeline doesn’t depend on their roadmap.”
- “The real differentiator isn’t a checklist; it’s whether you can *show* the controls and *export* the evidence. That’s where teams that built it late often struggle.”
- “We’ve seen deals slow down when the vendor can’t produce evidence by control or show a live view of control state. We built so that doesn’t happen.”

## SECTION 3 — Procurement Timeline Compression

**30-day enterprise acceleration plan**

---

### Week 1 — Foundation

| Step | Action | Owner |
|------|--------|--------|
| 1 | Send **Trust Center** link (public). Note: governance architecture, access control, audit, 4-eyes, incident, session/MFA, monitoring, DSAR, compliance, contact. | Sales |
| 2 | Send **One-Pager** (governance + control infrastructure). Include: segregation of duties, 4-eyes, tamper-evident audit, continuous monitoring, evidence-on-demand, escalation/SLA. | Sales |
| 3 | Send **FAQ** (one-page). Logging, role segregation, data request SLAs, incident response, audit exports, session governance. | Sales |

**Outcome:** Security/procurement have a single reference and quick answers. Fewer “send us a doc” loops.

---

### Week 2 — Demonstration

| Step | Action | Owner |
|------|--------|--------|
| 4 | Offer **live governance walkthrough** (20–30 min). Show: Trust Center, audit chain verification, 4-eyes flow, control health dashboard. Invite security/compliance. | Sales / SE |
| 5 | Provide **evidence export sample**. E.g. audit CSV (redacted), chain verification output, one control-code evidence pack. Include reproduce instructions. | Sales / Compliance |

**Outcome:** They see controls and evidence format. Review shifts from “do they have it?” to “how do we use it?”

---

### Week 3 — Close

| Step | Action | Owner |
|------|--------|--------|
| 6 | **Address open questions** using control mapping. Map their questions to: SOC 2, ISO 27001, GDPR (or their framework). Use evidence registry and control health per control. | Sales / Compliance |
| 7 | Re-share **Trust Center + FAQ** if new stakeholders join. Offer second walkthrough or Q&A if needed. | Sales |
| 8 | Confirm evidence-on-demand process and contact for ongoing audits. | Sales |

**Outcome:** No open governance blockers. Clear path for future audits and renewals.

---

**Summary:** Week 1 = share; Week 2 = show; Week 3 = map and close.

## SECTION 4 — Executive Buyer Framing

**Positioning for CFO / COO**

---

**Governance as risk discount reduction**  
“Our governance model is built into the product — segregation of duties, 4-eyes for destructive actions, tamper-evident audit, continuous control monitoring. That reduces the risk premium your security and legal teams attach to new vendors. We’re not asking you to accept a discount on controls; we’re giving you something you can verify. That often shows up as faster sign-off and lower perceived vendor risk.”

**Governance as operational predictability**  
“You get predictability: control health is visible, escalations are tracked, evidence is generated by control code. So you’re not dependent on one person or a manual process. That scales with your organization and with audits.”

**Governance as institutional readiness**  
“We’re built for institutional readiness — the kind of thing acquirers and investors look for. Control mapping, evidence registry, and a live view of control state. That supports diligence and reduces the ‘governance gap’ in later stages.”

**Governance as scalable oversight**  
“Oversight scales because it’s in the product. New regions, new compliance requirements, new audits — the same control model and evidence paths apply. You’re not layering process on top of a product that wasn’t designed for it.”

## SECTION 5 — Competitive Trap Strategy

**Subtle positioning: questions prospects can ask competitors**

---

Do not name competitors. Let the questions do the work. When prospects ask *you* these, you answer yes and show. When they ask *others*, the contrast becomes obvious.

**Suggested questions for prospects to use in their evaluation**

1. **“Do you have tamper-evident audit?”**  
   (Append-only, hash-chained log; verification and optional signed snapshots. Many have standard logging only.)

2. **“Do you require 4-eyes for destructive admin actions?”**  
   (Request → approve/reject by a second authority; logged. Many allow single-admin delete or bulk actions.)

3. **“Can you produce evidence by control code?”**  
   (Evidence mapped to SOC 2 / ISO / GDPR controls; generate by code; registry with timestamp and reproduce steps. Many offer generic exports or policy only.)

4. **“Do you have continuous control monitoring?”**  
   (Daily control health; RBAC consistency, audit chain validity, escalation age, session anomalies, overdue data requests; score and per-control status. Many have no live control view.)

**How to introduce the trap (subtle)**  
“When you’re comparing options, it’s worth asking how they handle audit integrity, who can approve destructive actions, how they produce evidence for specific controls, and whether they have a live view of control health. We’re happy to show you how we do each of those.”

**Outcome:** Prospect uses these as evaluation criteria. You meet them; others often don’t. No direct comparison required.
